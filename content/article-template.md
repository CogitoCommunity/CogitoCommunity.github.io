## SUMMARY BOX (Required – might skip for short articles) 
75-100 words \ 
Write a standalone summary that answers: \
What is this article about? \
What will the reader learn? \
Why does it matter? \
Example: \
 "This article explores how dopamine regulation affects student motivation and academic performance. By understanding the neuroscience of reward systems, students can design better study habits and avoid burnout. Practical takeaways include actionable strategies for managing screen time and optimizing focus."
  
## OPENING HOOK 
100-150 words \
Start with one of the following: \
A provocative question ("Why do we procrastinate even when we know it hurts us?") \
A surprising statistic ("70% of high school students report chronic sleep deprivation") \
A vivid anecdote or scenario \ 
A challenge to conventional wisdom \
Purpose: Make the reader care. Establish why this topic matters to students right now. \

## THESIS STATEMENT 
1-2 sentences \
State your core argument or insight. It should be: \
Specific (not "Sleep is important" but "Polyphasic sleep schedules may harm long-term memory consolidation") \
Debatable or Non-Obvious (if everyone already agrees, it's not worth writing) \
  
## BODY SECTIONS 
300-1200 words (divided into 2-4 subsections) \
Use descriptive headers (e.g., "The Neuroscience of Habit Formation" instead of "Body Paragraph 1"). \
Each subsection should: \
Present Evidence: Cite research, data, expert opinions, or real-world examples \
Analyze, Don't Summarize: Explain why this evidence matters and how it supports your thesis \
Connect to Your Argument: Every paragraph should ladder back to your main point \
Formatting Tips: \
Use bullet points or numbered lists sparingly (only when listing is clearer than prose) \
Include hyperlinks to sources within the text when referencing them 
Break up long paragraphs (aim for 3-5 sentences per paragraph) \

## CRITICAL ANALYSIS (Optional for 500-700 words, Required for 1000+) 
100-200 words \
This is NOT just "the other side of the argument." Instead, consider: \
Limitations: What doesn't this research account for? \
Counterarguments: What would a skeptic say? \
Nuance: Under what conditions might your thesis not hold true? \
Example: \
 "While dopamine-driven gamification can boost short-term motivation, critics argue it may undermine intrinsic interest in learning. Students might become dependent on external rewards, reducing long-term intellectual curiosity. This limitation is worth considering when designing study systems."
  
## REAL-WORLD APPLICATION 
100-150 words \
Answer: "So what? How can students actually USE this?" \
Provide: \
Concrete action steps \
Mindset shifts \
Tools or resources \
Questions for self-reflection \
Example: \
 "To apply this knowledge: (1) Track your dopamine triggers using a simple journal—what activities leave you energized vs. drained? (2) Replace passive scrolling with 'active consumption' (e.g., annotating articles instead of skimming). (3) Experiment with 25-minute focus blocks (Pomodoro) to optimize reward cycles."
 
## CONCLUSION 
75-100 words 
Restate your thesis in new words (don't copy-paste) 
End with a memorable closing line: 
A thought-provoking question 
A call to action 
A bold prediction or statement 
Avoid: Generic endings like "In conclusion, sleep is important for students." 
  
## CITATIONS (Minimum 2 Required) 
Format: Use APA 7 style \
Credibility Standards: ✓ Academic journals (PubMed, JSTOR, Google Scholar) \
 ✓ Reputable news outlets (NYT, BBC, Scientific American) \
 ✓ Primary sources (research papers, official reports, expert interviews) \
 ✗ Wikipedia, personal blogs, unverified websites \
Citation Examples: \
Journal: Smith, J. (2023). Dopamine and motivation in adolescents. Journal of Neuroscience, 45(3), 120-135. \
News: Brown, A. (2024, January 15). The science of procrastination. The New York Times. https://nytimes.com/article \
Book: Clear, J. (2018). Atomic Habits. Penguin Random House. 
Include hyperlinks for all online sources. 



#### FLEXIBILITY NOTE 
This template is a guide, not a straitjacket. If your article requires a different structure (e.g., an interview-based piece, a photo essay with commentary, a comparative analysis), that's fine—but explain your reasoning in your submission notes. 
  
## SUBMISSION CHECKLIST (Before You Submit) 
- Title is specific and engaging 
- Thesis is clear and debatable 
- At least 2 credible sources cited 
- Word count is 500-1500 words 
- Spellcheck and grammar check completed 
- All hyperlinks work 
- Real-world application section answers "So what?"
